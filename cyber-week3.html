Week 3: Advanced Simulations & Threat Hunting

Day 15 — Multi-Adversary Challenge

Goal: Simulate two or more different adversary profiles in quick succession or in a blended attack chain to stress-test both red team execution and blue team detection/response under chaotic, multi-attack conditions.

Step 1 — Select Adversary Profiles

  • Choose at least two real-world groups from MITRE ATT&CK:
    • Example 1: APT29 (Cozy Bear) — stealthy espionage operations.
    • Example 2: Conti Ransomware Group — aggressive, high-speed attacks.
  • Ensure chosen groups have distinct TTPs to create variety in attack patterns.

Step 2 — Map and Combine TTPs

  • Create an attack plan that includes:
    • Initial access methods from both actors.
    • Overlapping techniques (e.g., both using PowerShell but with different goals).
    • Distinct privilege escalation or persistence methods from each.
  • Decide if attacks will be:
    • Sequential: One adversary completes their chain before the next starts.
    • Concurrent: Both attack profiles operate at the same time.

Step 3 — Red Team Execution

  • Begin with adversary A's initial access and early-stage actions.
  • Introduce adversary B's activity before adversary A completes their chain.
  • Use different tooling, command syntax, and timing to make attribution harder.
  • Record all commands, timestamps, and hostnames affected.

Step 4 — Blue Team Response

  • Monitor for:
    • Increased alert volume due to multiple attack patterns.
    • Conflicting or overlapping IOCs.
    • Changes in attacker behavior as both sets of TTPs unfold.
  • Respond in real time by prioritizing high-impact detections.

Step 5 — Stress-Test Detection Rules

  • Evaluate whether:
    • Low-noise TTPs from adversary A still bypass detection.
    • High-noise TTPs from adversary B overwhelm analysts.
    • Alerts can be correctly attributed to the right simulated adversary.

Step 6 — Debrief and Lessons Learned

  • Red team: Document blended or conflicting tactics and their impact.
  • Blue team: Identify which detections failed due to noise or confusion.
  • Jointly decide on improvements to: Event correlation, Incident prioritization, Playbook flexibility under multi-threat conditions

End of Day 15 Success Criteria:

✅Two or more adversary profiles executed in blended or overlapping fashion
✅Blue team maintained detection and response without losing situational awareness
✅Documented improvements for handling complex, multi-actor scenarios

Day 16 — Endurance Simulation: Extended Operations

Goal: Conduct a continuous, 4–6 hour red vs. blue lab simulation with multiple intrusion attempts happening at unpredictable times to test both teams' sustained effectiveness and adaptability over extended operations.

Step 1 — Define Simulation Timeline

  • Schedule the exercise over a 4–6 hour window.
  • Plan 4–6 separate intrusion events, spaced irregularly (e.g., 20 minutes apart, then a 1-hour gap, then two quick attacks).
  • Assign different objectives to each intrusion: Persistence deployment, Privilege escalation, Lateral movement, Data exfiltration simulation, Stealth reconnaissance

Step 2 — Red Team Preparation

  • Develop varied attack plans for each event:
    • Mix high-noise and stealthy tactics.
    • Alternate between known and novel TTPs.
    • Use different user accounts, hosts, and persistence methods.
  • Randomize execution order so attacks are unpredictable.

Step 3 — Blue Team Setup

  • Verify logging (Sysmon, WEF, PowerShell) and alerting are active.
  • Prepare incident tracking sheets to record: Time of detection, Event ID(s), Response actions taken, Time to contain/eradicate threat
  • Ensure monitoring dashboards or SIEM queries are pre-loaded.

Step 4 — Execution

  • Red team begins attacks at pre-determined but concealed intervals.
  • Blue team actively monitors and responds in real time.
  • Maintain operational tempo without breaks to simulate fatigue factors.

Step 5 — Adaptation Mid-Exercise

  • Red team changes tactics mid-stream if a pattern is detected by blue team.
  • Blue team adjusts monitoring focus based on observed red team activity.
  • Both teams document decision-making under pressure.

Step 6 — Debrief and Analysis

  • Review: Total number of intrusion attempts detected vs. missed, Average detection and response times, Impact of fatigue on decision-making and reaction speed.
  • Identify: Most effective red team tactics under extended pressure, Blue team's strongest and weakest detection areas.
  • Update detection rules, response playbooks, and attack strategies accordingly.

End of Day 16 Success Criteria:

✅All planned intrusion events executed across the full time window
✅Blue team maintained operational monitoring without lapses
✅Detailed performance metrics captured for both teams
✅Updated tactics and playbooks reflecting lessons learned from extended operations

Day 17 — Proactive Threat Hunting

Goal: Conduct a proactive blue team hunt for indicators of compromise (IOCs) and malicious activity within lab.local without prior alerts from the red team, simulating a real-world scenario where defenders must identify stealth attacks on their own.

Step 1 — Establish a Threat Hunting Hypothesis

  • Define the scope and focus of the hunt:
    • Example: "An adversary has compromised a domain user account and is moving laterally."
  • Determine the types of activity you will look for:
    • Unusual logon patterns
    • Rare process executions
    • Suspicious PowerShell usage
    • Persistence indicators

Step 2 — Collect and Centralize Data

  • Pull logs from Sysmon, Event Viewer, and WEF.
  • Ensure visibility into: Security event logs, PowerShell operational logs, Sysmon process and network activity
  • If using a SIEM, load relevant log sources and create temporary hunting dashboards.

Step 3 — Search for Anomalies

  • Query for:
    • Event ID 4624 with rare logon types or odd workstations
    • Sysmon Event ID 1 for uncommon process executions
    • Sysmon Event ID 3 for unusual network connections
    • PowerShell logs with Base64-encoded commands
    • WMI events (Sysmon Event ID 21) tied to non-admin accounts

Step 4 — Investigate Leads

  • For each suspicious event:
    • Identify the originating host and account
    • Trace process ancestry
    • Check for related persistence mechanisms (e.g., scheduled tasks, registry keys)
  • Correlate with historical activity to rule out false positives.

Step 5 — Document Findings

  • Maintain a hunt log including: Event IDs, Hostnames and accounts, Timeline of suspicious activity, Determination: benign or malicious
  • Prioritize any confirmed malicious activity for containment.

Step 6 — Contain and Remediate

  • For confirmed malicious findings: Disable compromised accounts, Remove persistence mechanisms, Isolate affected hosts
  • Update detection rules to catch similar activity in the future

End of Day 17 Success Criteria:

✅Threat hunting hypothesis tested and investigated
✅At least one anomaly detected and analyzed
✅Updated detection coverage based on hunt results
✅Documented process for repeating proactive hunts

Day 18 — Adversary Persistence Hunt

Goal: Conduct a focused blue team operation to search for and eradicate stealthy, long-term persistence mechanisms in lab.local that could survive typical incident response, ensuring a clean and secure environment.

Step 1 — Define Persistence Hunt Scope

  • Focus on persistence techniques from MITRE ATT&CK categories such as:
    • Registry run keys and startup folders
    • WMI event subscriptions
    • Modified or rogue scheduled tasks
    • Malicious Group Policy logon scripts
    • Kerberos Golden Tickets or Silver Tickets

Step 2 — Collect and Review Data Sources

  • Pull logs from: Sysmon (Event IDs 1, 13, 21), Security Event Logs (Event IDs 4698, 4702, 4769), Directory Services logs for GPO changes (Event ID 5136)
  • If available, query historical logs in your SIEM for persistence-related events.

Step 3 — Hunt for Persistence Indicators

  • Search for:
    • Scheduled tasks created or modified by unexpected accounts.
    • WMI filters and consumers in root\subscription.
    • Unusual entries in: HKCU\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run, HKLM\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run, HKCU\Environment
    • Recently modified scripts in SYSVOL for GPOs.
    • Active Kerberos tickets with unusually long lifetimes.

Step 4 — Investigate Suspicious Findings

  • Confirm whether each indicator is malicious or legitimate.
  • For registry keys or scheduled tasks, verify creation dates and associated executables.
  • For Kerberos tickets, check issuance details and compare with normal authentication patterns.

Step 5 — Eradicate Malicious Persistence

  • Remove malicious scheduled tasks:
    schtasks /delete /tn "<taskname>" /f
  • Delete rogue WMI subscriptions: 
    Get-WmiObject -Namespace root\subscription -Class __EventFilter | Remove-WmiObject
Get-WmiObject -Namespace root\subscription -Class CommandLineEventConsumer | Remove-WmiObject
Get-WmiObject -Namespace root\subscription -Class __FilterToConsumerBinding | Remove-WmiObject
  • Clean malicious registry entries using reg delete.
  • Revert unauthorized GPO changes.
  • Invalidate Golden Tickets by resetting the KRBTGT account password twice.

Step 6 — Validate and Document

  • Re-run persistence hunting queries to confirm removal.
  • Log: Persistence methods found, Removal steps taken, Detection rules updated to catch similar future attempts

End of Day 18 Success Criteria:

✅All malicious persistence methods identified and removed
✅Detection rules updated for future coverage
✅Documentation completed with remediation steps and prevention recommendations

Day 19 — Offensive Persistence Re-Entry

Goal: Simulate a red team attempt to re-establish persistence in lab.local immediately after the blue team's Day 18 persistence hunt, testing whether updated defenses and detection rules can identify and block new, more creative footholds.

Step 1 — Review Blue Team Updates

  • Analyze detection rules and removal processes implemented on Day 18.
  • Identify blind spots or areas where persistence hunting coverage is weak.
  • Plan new persistence techniques that exploit these gaps.

Step 2 — Select Advanced Persistence Methods

  • Use less common techniques that are harder to detect:
    • Modifying legitimate scheduled tasks instead of creating new ones.
    • Leveraging obscure registry keys (e.g., HKCU\Environment variables).
    • Creating WMI event subscriptions with rare triggers.
    • Hiding payloads within legitimate binaries (living-off-the-land binaries).
    • Establishing GPO changes with minimal modifications to existing scripts.

Step 3 — Deploy Persistence Stealthily

  • Avoid high-noise actions that would trigger alerts from Day 18 tuning.
  • Spread deployments across multiple machines to increase survivability.
  • Randomize timing of persistence activation to avoid immediate detection.

Step 4 — Monitor Blue Team Response

  • As the red team, observe whether:
    • Persistence is detected through Sysmon, WEF, or PowerShell logging.
    • Alerts are generated in near real time.
    • Blue team isolates compromised machines or accounts promptly.

Step 5 — Blue Team Actions

  • Hunt for:
    • Modifications to existing scheduled tasks.
    • Registry changes outside previously monitored keys.
    • WMI filters/consumers with uncommon event queries.
    • Slight alterations to legitimate logon scripts.
  • Validate detection of persistence activation events.

Step 6 — Debrief and Adjust

  • Red team: Document persistence methods used, stealth measures applied, and which remained undetected.
  • Blue team: Identify which new techniques bypassed detection and update hunting/detection coverage.
  • Jointly define countermeasures to block future re-entry attempts.

End of Day 19 Success Criteria:

✅Red team deployed at least two creative persistence methods
✅Blue team detected and removed as many as possible
✅Detection rules expanded to cover newly tested techniques

Day 20 — Full-Scope Campaign Simulation

Goal: Conduct a multi-day, end-to-end red vs. blue simulation that incorporates reconnaissance, exploitation, privilege escalation, persistence, lateral movement, detection, hunting, and remediation — blending all skills from Days 1–19 into one continuous campaign.

Step 1 — Campaign Planning

  • Red Team:
    • Design an attack storyline that unfolds over several simulated days.
    • Include realistic dwell time between phases to mimic real adversaries.
    • Blend noisy and stealthy tactics, mixing prior techniques with new variations.
  • Blue Team:
    • Prepare monitoring infrastructure with tuned rules from prior days.
    • Establish an incident response timeline for the duration of the campaign.

Step 2 — Initial Access

  • Use one or more methods from previous exercises:
    • Phishing simulation (lab-safe payload)
    • Exploitation of a lab service
    • Compromised standard domain credentials

Step 3 — Reconnaissance and Enumeration

  • Map out lab.local using:
    • Low-noise commands and PowerShell scripts from Day 11
    • Target identification via BloodHound or manual queries

Step 4 — Privilege Escalation and Lateral Movement

  • Execute:
    • Kerberoasting or token abuse from Day 6
    • PsExec, WinRM, or WMI pivoting from Day 4
    • Movement to high-value targets (e.g., Domain Controller)

Step 5 — Persistence Deployment

  • Establish multiple persistence methods, including:
    • Registry keys, scheduled tasks, or WMI subscriptions
    • GPO modifications for domain-wide impact
    • One or more stealth persistence techniques from Day 19

Step 6 — Blue Team Threat Hunting and Response

  • Detect and disrupt:
    • Enumeration and escalation activities
    • Persistence deployments
    • Unusual logon patterns or privilege use
  • Use proactive hunts (Day 17) and persistence sweeps (Day 18)

Step 7 — Campaign Climax and Containment

  • Red team executes a final objective: Simulated data exfiltration, Ransomware-style encryption (lab-safe)
  • Blue team contains compromised systems and accounts.

Step 8 — Joint Debrief

  • Red team presents the full attack chain, including: Tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), Timing and stealth considerations
  • Blue team presents detection points, gaps, and response metrics.
  • Collaboratively identify improvements for: Detection coverage, Incident response processes, Adversary emulation playbooks

End of Day 20 Success Criteria:

✅Complete multi-phase campaign executed without breaking lab integrity
✅Blue team detected and responded to multiple phases
✅Documented lessons learned and updated defenses for future simulations

Day 21 — After-Action Review & Improvement Plan

Goal: Analyze the full Day 1–20 Windows hacking bootcamp, evaluate red and blue team performance, identify skill gains and gaps, and create a roadmap for future advanced scenarios.

Step 1 — Campaign Debrief Collection

  • Gather all logs, screenshots, BloodHound data, and notes from Days 1–20.
  • Compile red team attack chains, blue team detection points, and missed indicators.
  • Ensure both sides share full details of tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) used.

Step 2 — Performance Metrics Review

  • Measure: Red team success rate in achieving objectives, Blue team detection rate and mean time to detect (MTTD), Blue team mean time to respond (MTTR) and contain (MTTC), Number of false positives vs. true positives.
  • Compare to Day 1 baseline capabilities.

Step 3 — Skill Gap Identification

  • Red team: Identify areas where attacks were easily detected or failed.
  • Blue team: Identify undetected activities and delayed responses.
  • Prioritize gaps that had the largest operational impact.

Step 4 — Detection and Defense Updates

  • Based on findings:
    • Expand Sysmon and PowerShell logging coverage.
    • Adjust correlation rules for stealth techniques.
    • Strengthen incident response workflows.
  • Document new detection logic and distribute to the team.

Step 5 — Offensive Strategy Refinement

  • Develop new attack variations to bypass updated defenses.
  • Add novel persistence, escalation, and evasion methods to the red team playbook.
  • Plan for integrating real-world adversary TTPs from threat intelligence sources.

Step 6 — Roadmap for Next Training Cycle

  • Schedule next month's advanced simulation series.
  • Include: More complex multi-stage intrusions, Larger simulated enterprise environments, Integration of cloud/hybrid infrastructure into attack/defense scenarios.

End of Day 21 Success Criteria:

✅Comprehensive after-action report completed.
✅Metrics and skill gap analysis documented.
✅Updated detection and attack playbooks ready.
✅Clear roadmap created for future advanced training cycles.
